Forecasting Treasury Yields? (Bianchi et al., 2021)

Mapping yields with states

We start with the accounting identity of Campbell and Shiller (1991). We consider a zero-coupon bond with maturity t+n and a payoff of one dollar. We denote its (log) price and (continuously compounded) yield at time t by pt(n) and ytn respectively. The superscript refers to the bonds remaining maturity. The (log) excess return to the n-year bond from t to t+1, when its remaining maturity is n−1, is denoted by xrt+1(n).

(1)xrt+1(n)=pt+1(n−1)−pt(n)−yt(1)

For the relationship between bond yield and (log) price, we already know that:

(2)pt(n)=−n⋅yt(n)

Then, it is possible to express the log returns to bonds as:

(3)xrt+1(n)=pt+1(n−1)−pt(n)−yt(1)=−(n−1)⋅yt+1(n−1)+n⋅yt(n)−yt(1)=−(n−1)⋅(yt+1(n−1)−yt(n))+(yt(n)−yt(1))

The identity states that, after controlling for the slope yt(n)−yt(1), any variable that forecasts the change in the bond yield from t to t+1 (i.e., yt+1(n−1)−yt(n)) also forecasts the log returns to bonds (Bianchi et al., 2021).

Assuming that the investors information set at time t can be summarized by a latent k-dimensional state vector xt, we have the identity (with two parts) as follows according to Campbell and Shiller (1991):

yt(n)=1n∑j=0n−1Et(yt+j(1)∣xt)+1n∑j=0n−1Et(xrt+j+1(n−j)∣xt)
  • The first part represents the average expected short-term interest rates (one-period yields) from time t to t+n−1. In other words, it reflects the idea that long-term bond yields are determined by the average level of expected future short-term interest rates.
  • The second part represents the expected average of excess returns embedded in long-term yields. In other words, bond yields are not only determined by expected future short-term interest rates, but also reflect investors’ expectations of future risk premia (or excess returns).
  • All the expectations are conditional on the information set xt available to investors at time t (which includes latent state variables), reflecting their knowledge and expectations.

This identity can also be written as:

yt=f(xt;N)

where we stack time-t yields on bonds with different maturities in a vector yt, and the maturities of the bonds in the vector N. Every term structure model reduces to a specific mapping f between yields and state variables. Combining the equation above with equation (3), we obtain:

Et[xrt+1(n)]=g(xt;N)

Latent factors

yt represents the observed yield data, typically a high-dimensional vector composed of yields across different maturities of treasury bonds. xt denotes the state variables or latent factors , which are not directly observable and must be estimated from yt using statistical methods such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA). In the Affine Term Structure Models of Duffie & Kan (1996) and Dai & Singleton (2000), xt is a state vector that can capture "latent factors driving the yield curve". It can explain most of the variation in the term structure of interest rates and is commonly interpreted in economic terms as:

  • Level: the overall interest rate level
  • Slope: the difference between short-term and long-term interest rates
  • Curvature: the degree of bending in the yield curve

Yields-Only Framework

In the simplest case, yields are linear affine functions of the state variables:

yt=A+Bxt

The linearity of f(⋅), together with a dimensionality reduction of the space of yields yt, gives rise to principal component regression (PCR) where the quantity of interest (excess bond returns, xrt+1(n)) is regressed onto principal components xt (Friedman et al., 2001):

(4)Et[xrt+1(n)]=α^+β^⊤xtwithxt=Wyt+b

where the columns of W form an orthogonal basis for directions of greatest variance, and b captures the average reconstruction error or bias. The linear predictive system outlined in equation (4) represents a two-step procedure where researchers extract the latent factors xt, and then learn the regression coefficients by minimizing a loss function that depends on the residual sum of squares.

Macro plus Yields Framework

In addition to this yields-only specification, researchers have often evaluated the role of macroeconomic variables as an important driver of bond returns. This leads to an augmented predictive regression:

(5)Et[xrt+1(n)]=α^+β^⊤xt+γ^⊤Ft

The vector F represents a set of key factors selected from a large number of macroeconomic indicators that are most closely related to bond market performance (Ludvigson and Ng, 2009). Equations (3) and (4) constitute two important applications of unsupervised data compression for bond forecasting. And they all adopted the principal component regression (PCR) model.

Part 1 Macro Scenario Stress Testing

1. Variables

In conducting stress testing—particularly when establishing a stress transmission model—the macroeconomic variables used are not necessarily predetermined. Rather, following the approach of Breuer et al. (2008), we employ a method that minimizes the Mahalanobis distance, using the stress scenarios specified by the People's Bank of China as a foundation. By leveraging the correlations among macro variables, we construct the macro stress scenario used in our model. We assume that this result effectively captures the propagation of stress across the macro variables.

2. Models

2.1 Corporate Stress Testing Model

2.1.1 PD Model

The internal credit rating system for non‑retail clients operates on a through‑the‑cycle (TTC) model and does not incorporate macroeconomic variables. To perform stress testing, we take the TTC model outcomes as a baseline and introduce macroeconomic shocks to construct a point‑in‑time (PIT) model that propagates the macro stress scenario into client ratings.

For real estate–related loans, due to the low incidence of defaults, we attempted a separate model. However, the results were not satisfactory. Consequently, for real estate exposures, our stress testing utilizes the same model framework as that used for macro scenario stress testing.

In building the model, we utilize the bank’s client sample spanning from 20** to 201**. For performing exposures that did not default during the sample period, we align each client’s rating with macroeconomic variables observed one year prior to the rating date. For defaulted clients, we align the client’s rating with macro variables observed one year prior to the default date, and use the rating from that corresponding point. Because the number of non‑defaulted clients far exceeds that of defaulted ones, we sample the non‑default population at a ratio of 3:1 relative to defaulted clients to construct the modeling dataset.

Because the bank’s system allows for calculation of a through‑the‑cycle PD from the rating score for each client, we then apply a logistic transformation to the PD derived from our model:

R=ln(PD/(1−PD))

The variable in question is included in the model as one of the predictors. We then employ a logit regression framework. The resulting form of the default probability model is:

PD=11+exp(−(c0+c1∗R+∑i=13ci+1∗Mi))

Mi(i=1...4) represents the macroeconomic variables included in the model, which are, respectively: GDP, Total credit volume, Year‑on‑year growth rate of GDP in the current quarter, Year‑on‑year growth rate of average commodity housing sales price. ci(i=1...4) represents the corresponding parameters estimated from the regression.

Since the purpose of this regression model is primarily to predict whether matched clients default or not, the model output is only useful for ranking client creditworthiness — it cannot directly reflect actual default probabilities. Therefore, it is necessary to further adjust the model parameters for calibration. Because all non-defaulted sample clients in the modeling dataset have observed PDs that are already calibrated actual values, we use these as benchmarks to calibrate the model’s predicted PDs. Recognizing that the logit‑transformed PDs satisfy a linear relationship, we calibrate the model via the following adjustment:

ln(PDr1−PDr)=e0+e1ln(PDm1−PDm)

Here, PDr denotes the actual (observed) default probability, while PDm represents the model‑predicted PD. The regression yields calibration parameters e0，e1, where e0=−1.∗∗∗ and e1=0.∗∗∗. Combining the two stages of modeling, the final point‑in‑time PD is calculated using the following formula:

PD=11+exp(−(e0+e1∗(c0+c1∗R+∑i=13ci+1∗Mi)))

Since the bank’s master scale takes cyclical effects into account, the adjusted model can calculate default rates that, while incorporating cyclical effects, accurately reflect the impact of stress. Using this model, we can calculate the stressed PD value for each individual debt exposure on a loan-by-loan basis.

2.1.2 LGD Model

A bank has developed an internal rating LGD model, which can directly use macroeconomic scenarios to predict the mean LGD and set the model parameters based on the mean LGD. In practice, according to regulatory requirements, the calculation of LGD takes into account the impact of economic downturn periods, using the mean LGD under the worst historical macroeconomic scenario to set the model parameters.

Using the mean prediction model, we can obtain the predicted mean LGD under the overall credit risk macroeconomic scenarios for mild, moderate, and severe stress cases, which are 0.4***, 0.6***, and 0.7***, respectively; while the bank’s existing model adopts a mean LGD of 0.5***. Therefore, under the mild stress scenario, we will still use the currently adopted mean LGD for economic downturns to calculate LGD, while under the moderate and severe stress scenarios, we will use the more severe means obtained from the calculations. When calculating LGD, depending on the mild, moderate, and severe scenarios, we multiply the value of real estate collateral by 0.9, 0.8, and 0.7, respectively, and then input the adjusted values into the corresponding model to obtain the stressed LGD results.

Under the macroeconomic scenarios for credit risk in the real estate-related industries, the predicted mean LGD for mild, moderate, and severe stress scenarios are 0.5***, 0.7***, and 0.8***, respectively; while the bank’s existing model adopts a mean LGD of 0.5***. Therefore, under all three stress scenarios, we will use the more severe means obtained from the calculations to determine LGD. When calculating LGD, depending on the mild, moderate, and severe scenarios, we multiply the value of real estate collateral by 0.9, 0.8, and 0.6, respectively, and then input the adjusted values into the corresponding model to obtain the stressed LGD results.

2.1.3 Determination of Non-Performing Loans

Based on the stressed PD and LGD results calculated in the previous section, as well as the bank’s debt exposure information as of the end of 20**, we can determine the stressed default rate for each debt exposure according to the rating of the actual risk bearer, and link it to the corresponding loss given default. Using these linked results, we can then calculate the expected loss rate under different stress scenarios.
